Housing in Cambridge

Anyone else remember this guy?

Ask any candidate for city council about their priorities for the city and you’re likely to hear something about affordable housing and development. A perennial subject of debate in Cambridge politics, the issue of housing includes the complicating factors of university housing, a booming tech economy, and the space limitations of any urban environment. A new apartment building can signal positive signs of growth or unwanted gentrification, depending on your perspective.

By the numbers
  • More than 30% of Cambridge residents attend school full- or part-time, with more than 6,000 students living in off-campus housing
  • The median income is over $100k, but 14% of individuals and 9% of families live below the federal poverty line
  • In the last 20 years, Cambridge has seen population growth at the highest income levels (7.5% increase/year) while losing middle-income residents (6% decrease/year)
  • Median monthly rent citywide is over $3,000/month, and homes sell for over a million
  • The Cambridge Community Development Department administers affordable housing programs for renters and buyers, but wait times are unpredictable and can be long
  • In April 2017, the Cambridge city council voted unanimously to increase the required amount of affordable units in new development from 15% to 20%
  • Sources: Cambridge Housing Profile 2016, Community Development Department News

Cambridge faces high housing prices as demand and local incomes rise, which can price out long-time residents. Possible changes to the current system attempt to address both the supply and demand sides of this equation. Graduate students at MIT filed a petition in August 2017 to require the university to build more student housing before developing commercial space in Kendall Square. Other proposals further change affordable housing policy, including adding middle-income programs and larger units for families. A transfer tax would penalize real estate speculators who buy property without living in it.

Another hot-button topic around this issue has been campaign funding from local developers. Some citizens worry that taking this funding will skew candidates’ priorities when writing city policy.
